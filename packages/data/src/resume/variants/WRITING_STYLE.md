# Writing Style Guide

This file captures the durable writing standards for resume variants and cover letters in this folder.

These rules are the house style. External articles may inform them, but agents should follow this file rather than trying to imitate outside sources directly.

## Core Principles

- Sound like a real person, not a ceremonial document.
- Be clear, direct, and specific.
- Prefer sharp, legible writing over polished-but-generic writing.
- Keep the voice senior and credible, not theatrical.
- Preserve factual integrity at all times.

## Resume Style

- Tailor lightly unless the prompt explicitly asks for stronger adaptation.
- Keep the base identity intact and shift emphasis rather than rewriting the whole story.
- Favor evidence over adjectives.
- Surface:
  - scope
  - outcomes
  - leadership context
  - business impact
  - technical depth
- Use keywords naturally and sparsely.
- If a term like `generalist`, `AI-first`, or `operator` is useful, weave it in lightly and only where it feels earned.

## Cover Letter Style

- A cover letter should read like deliberate written communication, not a formality.
- It should feel specific to the opportunity, not mass-produced.
- Keep it concise.
- Usually 3-4 short paragraphs is enough.
- Make it easy to understand:
  - why this role is relevant
  - why this background fits
  - what kind of operator/builder/leader this person is
- Avoid reciting the entire resume.
- Avoid generic “I am excited to apply” filler unless the prompt explicitly calls for a more traditional tone.
- Do not sound needy, breathless, or over-eager.

## Tone

- Confident, not inflated
- Warm, not chatty
- Professional, not stiff
- Specific, not broad
- Human, not “AI-brown”

## What To Avoid

- keyword stuffing
- generic recruiter cliches
- exaggerated self-praise
- vague claims without evidence
- copy that sounds like it could be sent unchanged to 100 employers
- overly ornate or performative phrasing

## Good Signals

- visible effort
- care and diligence
- clear thinking
- internal organization
- concise communication
- obvious relevance to the target role

## Personalization Rules

- Personalize to the role, company, or audience when useful.
- Do not overfit if the variant may be forwarded broadly.
- If the user asks for a lightly tailored version, prioritize relevance without making it sound employer-exclusive.

## Distilled Cover Letter Rules

These are the specific principles to preserve when writing cover letters in this repo:

- The cover letter is often the first real writing sample.
- It should create a strong first signal of effort and care.
- It should quickly tell whether this person wants this role, not just any role.
- It should make clear why this person wants this specific job, not merely a similar category of job.
- It should be readable enough that someone would want to keep reading.
- It should reveal personality through clarity and judgment, not gimmicks.
- It should be personal and fluent enough to prove the writer can communicate well in writing.
- The best letters are not too long, not too short, and not interchangeable.

## How To Use This File

- Read this before drafting or revising any variant.
- Use the prompt for role-specific direction.
- Use `REFERENCES.md` only for background context and citation purposes.
